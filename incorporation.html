Why Delaware?

Delaware corporate law is THE body of corporate statutory and case law with which the greatest number of lawyers and investors are familiar. With years and years of corporate cases in Delaware the majority of players have greater confidence in how Delaware courts will address a wide range of business or governance disputes. Companies, VCs, lawyers can be sure what to expect from Delaware corporate law and that is a great advantage of forming your company in the state.

Clarity and predictability is definitely a win but let’s not forget about tax benefits that the state offers. According to Delaware tax law the state doesn't impose income tax on corporations registered in the state which don't do business in the state. Besides, shareholders not residing in Delaware don’t have to pay tax on shares in the state.

Combining the robustness of Delaware corporate law and tax benefits, Delaware is considered THE state for Corporations.

    1 – Choose a Name for your Business

    The name should be unique to the Delaware State

    You can check if the name is already taken at the Delaware's Division of Corporations website here: conduct a name search.

    If you find the name of your business is available, you may reserve an entity name

    Reserve the name online or submit a reservation application in the mail, pay a fee of $75, and your business name will be held for 120 days.

    2 – Designate a Registered Agent in Delaware

    Delaware requires that you designate a registered agent for your business

    A registered agent acts as the state's means to communicate with a business and is responsible for receiving legal and official documents related to the business. A registered agent can be anyone who has a physical address in Delaware. A P.O. Box is not allowed as a registered agent address.

    What it is. Why is it filed? How [selling point]

    • General purpose clause
    • List of total number of authorized shares for the corporation and the value assigned to each stock
    • Name and mailing address of the Delaware corporation's incorporator

    5 – Appoint your Corporate Directors

    The incorporator can designate the initial directors if there are no initial directors named in the certificate of incorporation. This is done by creating the "Statement of Incorporator" which will list the names and addresses of the initial directors of the business.

    The statement of incorporator is then signed by all of the incorporators. This is not filed with the state, but is kept as part of the corporate record in a corporate minute book.

    6 – Hold the First Meeting of the Board of Directors

    The first meeting of the board of directors is an important time in the life cycle of a corporation

    During this meeting, the initial directors of the business will adopt the corporate bylaws, set the fiscal year, and appoint corporate officers. It is also common to authorize the issuance of shares of stock to the founders. This meeting must be recorded in corporate minutes.

    Minutes are documents that detail what was discussed and any decisions the business makes during meetings.

    Corporate minutes are required for all Delaware corporations by law. Falling behind on keeping and maintaining minutes can jeopardize the corporation's liability protection and overall tax advantages. It is important to maintain updated corporate minutes and keep copies of all the signed and approved minutes or actions by unanimous consent from any special or annual meetings of the corporation's shareholders and directors.

    7 – Authorize the Issuance of Shares of Stock

    You can set the value to each stock however you see fit in your corporate bylaws

    For example, one share could be worth $10 or $10,000, either would be completely acceptable. Same situation with the number of shares - it can be any number. The most common approach is to issue 10-15 million shares with 0.0001 or 0.00001 price per share. The cheaper the initial stock, the easier it is for the founders to buy their shares.

    8 – Get an EIN Number for your Business

    An EIN (also referred to as a Federal Tax ID) is a nine-digit number that is issued by the IRS and used to uniquely identify your business for tax purposes.

    For that reason, filing for an EIN is one of the last things to do when you are setting up a business.

    In Delaware, nearly all industries require registering and licensing your business. Begin your business license registration at Delaware One Stop.

    10 – Open Bank Account

    The best time to open a business bank account is during the incorporation process. A bank account cannot be opened until the business has a license to operate and an EIN.

    11 – Buy Your Stock

    Failure to buy a stock in time is one of the crucial mistakes that founders make when setting up the company

    Once you’ve registered the company, organized the bylaws it is time to buy your stock under Delaware General Corporation Law, Section 152. On the first meeting of the board of directors the decision about purchasing shares should be authorized. After the approval from the board, the founder needs to sign a common stock purchase agreement.

    You must file a form called «Election under section 83 (B) of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986» (or similar) within 30 days of purchasing the shares

    There is no option to file this form later, so the 30 days term should be strictly observed. The form should be filed by mailing it by certified mail, return receipt requested to the IRS Service Center where you file your tax returns. See www.irs.gov.

    The 83 (B) election is a provision under the Internal Revenue Code (IRC) that gives an employee, or startup founder, the option to pay taxes on the total fair market value of restricted stock at the time of granting.

    12 – File Annual Report and Pay Franchise Tax

    All corporations incorporated in Delaware must file an Annual Report online at corp.delaware.gov/paytaxes/ no later than March 1st of each year.

    The Delaware Annual Report filing fee for a non-exempt domestic corporation is $50.00 per year. In addition to filing fees Delaware corporations must pay Delaware Franchise Tax by March 1st each year.

    There are two methods of calculation: Authorized shares method and Assumed par value. A corporation has a choice as to which method to use.

    Authorized shares method

    For corporations having no par value stock the Authorized Shares Method will always result in the lesser tax.

    • 5,000 shares or less (minimum tax) $175.00
    • 5,001 - 10,000 shares $250.00
    • Each additional 10,000 shares or portion thereof, add $85.00
    • Hire employees in Delaware
    • Maximum annual tax is $200,000.00

    For example:

    A corporation with 10,005 shares authorized pays $250.00 + $85.00= $335
    A corporation with 100,000 shares authorized pays $250.00 + $85.00 x 9= $1015

    Assumed par value method

    Assumed par value is the Company’s gross assets divided by all of its issued and outstanding shares.

    Many are tempted to simply authorize a small number of shares. Under the Authorized Shares Method, any number 5,000 or fewer results in only $175.00 in annual franchise tax. But this generally doesn't work long for companies planning to raise substantial outside capital or to grant employee stock options. Many companies intending to go the VC funding route tend to authorize around 10 million shares from the outset.

    Consult the Delaware Franchise Tax Calculator for details. The penalty for not filing a completed annual report on or before March 1st is $100. Interest at 1.5% per month is applied to any unpaid tax balance. Foreign corporations are assessed a penalty of $125 if the annual report is not filed.

    The annual report is filed online. A Notification of Annual Report and Franchise Taxes due is sent to all Delaware registered agents in December of each year.

    You can find Q&As regarding Delaware Franchise Tax here: delawareinc.com/delaware-franchise-tax/#3

    14 – Transfer IP Rights

    The founder owns the IP to her work when she creates it

    Ownership in IP (trademarks, copyrights, trade secrets, and patents) does not automatically transfer to a company, even when the creator becomes an owner of the company.

    The only way to transfer the IP to the company is for the founder to sign and deliver an assignment of intellectual property rights to the company. This could be done at the founding of the new company by having the founder transfer ownership of the IP in exchange for her initial equity in the company. If a copyright, trademark, or patent was registered before the Corporation was formed, then the transfer will need to be recorded in the Copyright or Patent & Trademark Office.

    15 – Register Trademarks

    Registering your business name for a trademark isn't required in order for you to have trademark rights

    However, a registered trademark can provide greater protection for your brand while helping you build your brand and drive business growth.

    If you want to trademark your business, brand or product name, file with the United States Patent and Trademark office once you’ve formed your business.

    16 – Patent Your Product (If Needed)

    There are differences between utility patents and design patents

    Design patents protect how an invention looks while utility patents protect how an invention works. Ask yourself whether you want to protect how your concept looks or how it works, or both. Utility patents cost significantly more than design patents, and involve a much longer prosecution process.

    There is a first-to-file rule, which means it doesn’t matter how long ago you came up with an idea

    You need to secure an earlier patent filing date before others beat you to it. There is a 1-year grace period according to US patent laws, which means that you have only one year from the earliest date of public disclosure to file a US patent application.

    Utility patents can be very challenging to obtain, so don’t expect a quick and inexpensive process

    Instead, anticipate that you will get rejected multiple times if you want a stronger patent with broader claims. Expect a high rate of rejection and years of back-and-forth with the Patent Office.
